# Ondansetron and 5-HT3 antagonists

Ondansetron has a clear repurposing rationale from animal work, but two randomized human studies did not establish prevention of acute opioid tolerance or physical dependence, and neither studied cancer pain. ([British Journal of Pharmacology][2]; [Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine][3]; [Drug and Alcohol Dependence][4])

## What it is

Ondansetron is a selective 5-HT3-receptor antagonist indicated for prevention of nausea and vomiting associated with chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and surgery. ([DailyMed][1])

A rat experiment reported effects of ondansetron on morphine tolerance and dependence, creating the repurposing hypothesis later tested in people. ([British Journal of Pharmacology][2])

## Human evidence

A secondary analysis of a prospective double-blind randomized trial included 86 patients undergoing elective cesarean delivery with spinal anesthesia containing fentanyl and morphine. Forty-four received 8 mg intravenous ondansetron and 42 received placebo. Ondansetron "did not significantly affect postoperative pain scores or opioid consumption" during the first 24 hours. This was an acute surgical model after intrathecal opioid exposure, not chronic cancer pain. ([Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine][3])

A second double-blind randomized trial included 48 patients with chronic low-back pain. Participants received sustained-release morphine for 30 days plus either ondansetron 8 mg three times daily or placebo, followed by naloxone-precipitated withdrawal. Withdrawal occurred in both groups, with no significant between-group difference in objective or subjective withdrawal scores. The trial measured physical dependence in noncancer pain, not analgesic durability in cancer pain. ([Drug and Alcohol Dependence][4])

These two randomized studies do not support a broad claim that ondansetron prevents opioid tolerance or dependence in people, and they provide no direct cancer-pain efficacy evidence. ([Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine][3]; [Drug and Alcohol Dependence][4])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

The two human models differed from chronic cancer pain, but that mismatch is not evidence of benefit in the target population. The verdict would change only with a prespecified clinical phenotype, a credible exposure and timing rationale, and a trial measuring sustained analgesia, opioid exposure, function, and toxicity. ([Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine][3]; [Drug and Alcohol Dependence][4])

## Verdict

**Deprioritized.** The available randomized human evidence is negative in adjacent populations, and no direct cancer-pain efficacy result is established by these studies. ([Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine][3]; [Drug and Alcohol Dependence][4])
