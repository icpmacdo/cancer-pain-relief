# Ibudilast

Ibudilast has human clinical experience in other indications and a plausible neuroimmune rationale, but published human studies do not establish durable analgesic benefit in cancer pain. ([Neuropsychopharmacology][1]; [Systematic review][3])

## What it is

Ibudilast, also known as AV411 or MN-166, is a nonselective phosphodiesterase inhibitor that has been used clinically in Asia for asthma and poststroke dizziness. Animal research suggests that it can inhibit glial activation and alter opioid-mediated effects, including analgesia and withdrawal. ([Neuropsychopharmacology][1])

The proposed pain rationale is that neuroimmune and glial signaling can contribute to pain pathophysiology and opioid-related adaptations. The 2025 review described ample preclinical evidence but found no convincing clinical efficacy for current glial targets in pain treatment or prevention. ([Neuropsychopharmacology][1]; [Systematic review][3])

## Human evidence

One double-blind study tested ibudilast in 11 recently detoxified men with opioid dependence and no cancer-pain diagnosis. Some oxycodone-associated subjective pain ratings improved, but results were inconsistent across analgesia measures; the study primarily assessed subjective, reinforcing, and experimental-pain effects rather than sustained clinical pain control. ([Neuropsychopharmacology][1])

A separate inpatient pilot enrolled 45 opioid-dependent volunteers, of whom 31 completed the study. Participants were maintained on morphine and randomized among placebo and two ibudilast doses. The 40 mg twice-daily group showed greater oxycodone-associated analgesia on subjective pain ratings, but the result came from a cold-pressor laboratory paradigm rather than a cancer-pain durability trial. ([Drug and Alcohol Dependence][2])

A 2025 systematic review included 26 trials with 2,132 participants, including three ibudilast trials. Six trials were positive, 11 had mixed findings, and nine showed no effect across the glial-modulator class. Its conclusion was that the review "does not provide convincing evidence of efficacy" for current glial targets in pain treatment or prevention. ([Systematic review][3])

Neither of the two opioid laboratory studies above enrolled patients for cancer pain, and neither measured durable analgesia during long-term cancer treatment. ([Neuropsychopharmacology][1]; [Drug and Alcohol Dependence][2])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

The existing human evidence uses experimental pain in people with opioid dependence, not a defined cancer-pain or suspected opioid-induced-hyperalgesia population. A verdict-changing study would need a prespecified clinical phenotype, methods to distinguish that phenotype from cancer progression and withdrawal, and sustained measures of pain, opioid exposure, function, and toxicity. ([Neuropsychopharmacology][1]; [Drug and Alcohol Dependence][2]; [Systematic review][3])

## Verdict

**Watchlist.** The neuroimmune rationale and small laboratory signals justify continued observation, but the systematic review and absence of target-population evidence do not support ibudilast as a general cancer-pain add-on. ([Systematic review][3])
