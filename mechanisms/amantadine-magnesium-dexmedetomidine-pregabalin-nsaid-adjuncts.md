# Amantadine, magnesium, dexmedetomidine, pregabalin, and NSAID adjuncts

These drugs belong to an acute perioperative opioid-sparing lane studied through pain and opioid outcomes measured mainly during the first 24 to 48 postoperative hours. That population and time horizon differ from loss of analgesic durability during long-term cancer-pain treatment. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1])

## What it is

This cluster includes dexmedetomidine, clonidine, amantadine, magnesium, pregabalin, NSAIDs, and related adjuncts examined as perioperative strategies for opioid-induced hyperalgesia. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1]) The trials aimed to reduce postoperative pain, pain sensitization, opioid exposure, or opioid-related adverse effects after opioid-based anaesthesia, often involving remifentanil. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1])

## Human evidence

A 2023 network meta-analysis included 33 randomized trials and 1,711 patients. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1]) Amantadine ranked highest for postoperative pain, with a standardized mean difference of -3.06 versus placebo (95% CI -4.67 to -1.45) and a SUCRA ranking of 96.2%. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1]) The authors concluded, "Dexmedetomidine was the only intervention that outperformed placebo in all indicators." ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1])

The findings need substantial qualification. Several interventions had data from only one study, and the authors warned about small samples, possible bias, overestimated effects, and variation in sex, opioid dose, timing, regimen, surgery duration, and anaesthetic maintenance. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1]) The primary pain outcome was pain at rest 24 hours after surgery, not chronic cancer-pain durability. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

This is a scope mismatch, not evidence that the perioperative strategy failed. The meta-analysis measured postoperative pain, opioid use, and related outcomes over hours to days, so it cannot establish preservation of opioid analgesia over months in palliative care. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1]) The verdict would change if a chronic cancer-pain study measured sustained pain control, opioid escalation, toxicity, function, and treatment burden over a clinically meaningful interval.

## Verdict

**Adjunct lane.** The evidence supports further perioperative comparison, but it is not evidence for a general anti-tolerance treatment in chronic cancer pain because the included studies were acute surgical trials. ([Frontiers in Pharmacology][1])
