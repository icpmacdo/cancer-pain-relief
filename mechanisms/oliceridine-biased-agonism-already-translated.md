# Oliceridine and translated biased agonism

Oliceridine is a translation-caution case: a G-protein-bias development hypothesis reached US approval for intravenous acute pain, but the product label does not establish durable cancer-pain analgesia or removal of opioid-class risks. ([DailyMed][1]; [Trends in Pharmacological Sciences][3])

## What it is

Oliceridine is an intravenous mu-opioid-receptor agonist that was developed as a G-protein-biased ligand. The original hypothesis proposed that favoring G-protein signaling over beta-arrestin recruitment could preserve analgesia while reducing opioid adverse effects. ([Trends in Pharmacological Sciences][3])

## Human evidence

The US label indicates oliceridine for acute pain in adults when the pain is severe enough to require an intravenous opioid and alternatives are inadequate. It carries boxed warnings for opioid risks, including addiction, abuse, misuse, life-threatening respiratory depression, and concomitant use with benzodiazepines or other central nervous system depressants. The cumulative daily dose must not exceed 27 mg, and the label states that safety "beyond 48 hours of use was not evaluated" in controlled trials. ([DailyMed][1])

These labeled data address short-term intravenous acute-pain treatment, not sustained analgesic durability during long-term cancer treatment. ([DailyMed][1])

Trevena reported that it discontinued sale of the remaining OLINVYK strengths effective December 31, 2024. The filing states that the decision was made for business and financial reasons, was not due to efficacy or safety issues, and was not prompted by an FDA request. Commercial discontinuation therefore should not be misrepresented as an FDA safety withdrawal or a negative efficacy trial. ([Trevena SEC filing][2])

The wider beta-arrestin explanation has also been reassessed. A critical review reported that foundational beta-arrestin-2 knockout findings "have not been reproduced" and that assay amplification confounded some apparent signaling bias. ([Trends in Pharmacological Sciences][3]) A separate pharmacology study found low intrinsic efficacy for oliceridine, PZM21, and SR-17018 and concluded that low efficacy could explain their improved side-effect profiles. ([Science Signaling][4])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

Oliceridine answered a short-term acute-pain question, not a long-term analgesic-durability question. Reopening this lane for cancer pain would require a candidate with reproducible mechanistic separation, chronic-exposure safety evidence, and a trial centered on sustained pain control, function, toxicity, and opioid escalation. ([DailyMed][1]; [Trends in Pharmacological Sciences][3]; [Science Signaling][4])

## Verdict

**Translation caution and low priority.** The acute-pain approval does not show that biased agonism solved opioid tolerance, and the manufacturer-discontinued product is not an active long-term cancer-pain option. ([DailyMed][1]; [Trevena SEC filing][2]; [Trends in Pharmacological Sciences][3])
