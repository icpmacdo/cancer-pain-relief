# Oliceridine / biased agonism already translated

Oliceridine is the cautionary translation case for beta-arrestin / G-protein bias. It shows that an elegant receptor-level story can reach FDA approval and still fail to become a clean anti-tolerance or safer-opioid breakthrough.

## What it is

FDA-approved biased mu-opioid agonist for acute pain.

The beta-arrestin story is the most famous version of receptor-level optimism. The hope was that mu-opioid receptor activation could be steered toward G-protein signaling, preserving analgesia, while avoiding beta-arrestin-mediated pathways thought to contribute to tolerance and adverse effects. In animal work, beta-arrestin-2 knockout mice showed less morphine tolerance, which made the concept look like a route to safer opioids.

This gave rise to G-protein-biased mu-opioid agonists, with oliceridine as the most clinically important translation.

## Human evidence

It reached the clinic, but it still carries opioid-class respiratory/addiction warnings; Trevena reduced commercial support in 2024 and discontinued remaining OLINVYK sales as of Dec. 31, 2024 for business/financial reasons. ([ASCPT][1])

Oliceridine reached FDA approval for short-term IV treatment of severe acute pain, so this was not just academic speculation. But it did not become the clean "opioid without opioid liabilities" that the early narrative implied. It still carried opioid-class warnings for addiction, misuse, and life-threatening respiratory depression. Commercially, Trevena later discontinued remaining OLINVYK sales for business and financial reasons.

## Tractability verdict

**Cautionary case.** Shows that "biased agonism" did not straightforwardly deliver the clean anti-tolerance/safer-opioid win.

For this report, biased agonism is a cautionary case. It shows that even a mechanism with elegant genetic support, intense medicinal chemistry, and actual FDA approval can fail to become a practical anti-tolerance or safer-opioid breakthrough. The lesson is not "ignore receptor pharmacology." The lesson is "do not fund receptor pharmacology just because the animal story sounds like the holy grail."
