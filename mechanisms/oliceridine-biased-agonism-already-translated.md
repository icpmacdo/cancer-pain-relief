# Oliceridine / biased agonism already translated

The cautionary translation case for beta-arrestin / G-protein bias: an elegant receptor story that reached FDA approval and still did not deliver a clean anti-tolerance or safer-opioid win.

## What it is

An FDA-approved G-protein-biased mu-opioid agonist for short-term IV severe acute pain. The hope, seeded by reduced morphine tolerance in beta-arrestin-2-knockout mice, was to steer the receptor toward G-protein signaling that preserves analgesia while avoiding the beta-arrestin pathways thought to drive tolerance and side effects.

## Human evidence

It reached the clinic but still carries opioid-class warnings for addiction, misuse, and life-threatening respiratory depression; it did not become the clean "opioid without opioid liabilities" the early narrative implied. Trevena reduced commercial support and discontinued remaining OLINVYK sales for business/financial reasons. ([ASCPT][1])

*Add: the FDA/DailyMed label as the primary source (the 27 mg daily cap and no controlled data beyond 48h). Note: the dated "as of Dec 31, 2024" discontinuation could not be confirmed against a primary source in the main report; the defensible claim is "commercial support pulled / strategic review."*

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

The critical reassessment is that the modest oliceridine window is better explained by low intrinsic efficacy / partial agonism than by bias, and the key beta-arrestin-2-knockout results have not reproduced. The lesson is not "ignore receptor pharmacology" but "do not fund it just because the animal story sounds like the holy grail."

## Verdict

**Graveyard.** A cautionary case, not a solved problem; no active clinical anti-tolerance path.
