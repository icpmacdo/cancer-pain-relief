# Buprenorphine rotation

The simplest version of opioid tolerance is: repeated opioid exposure makes the same dose produce less analgesia. But "anti-tolerance" is not one intervention class. Buprenorphine rotation belongs to the clinical-workaround family: not a molecular cure, but a possible way to regain analgesic control or improve safety when long-term full-agonist opioids are no longer working well.

## What it is

Switch long-term full agonist opioid patients to buprenorphine, a partial mu-agonist with possible antihyperalgesic properties.

Opioid rotation is not a molecular cure for tolerance. It is a clinical workaround. Patients who tolerate or respond poorly to one opioid may respond better to another because tolerance is incomplete across drugs. That can matter enormously in palliative care, where the practical goal is not mechanistic elegance but getting the patient back under the relief target.

Buprenorphine rotation is a second version of this story. Buprenorphine is a partial mu-agonist with high receptor affinity and a different safety/pharmacology profile from full agonists.

## Human evidence

A 2021 systematic review of 22 studies found low-quality evidence that buprenorphine rotation reduced pain without precipitating withdrawal or serious adverse events. ([JAMA Network][1])

In chronic pain patients on long-term opioids, systematic review evidence suggests rotation can reduce pain without commonly precipitating withdrawal or serious adverse events, but the evidence quality is low. This makes it interesting but not cleanly proven.

## Tractability verdict

**Potentially practical**, especially chronic pain/OUD overlap. But evidence quality is low and it is more "safer opioid strategy" than anti-tolerance.

This is more chronic-pain/OUD-adjacent than palliative-care-specific, but it is practical. It belongs among the more fundable anti-tolerance-adjacent bets when the target population includes pain, safety, dependence, and opioid-use-risk overlap.
