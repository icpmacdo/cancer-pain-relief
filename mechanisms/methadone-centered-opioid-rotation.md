# Methadone-centered strategies

The best-supported near-term opioid strategy in this research landscape for opioid-refractory cancer pain, but not an established anti-tolerance treatment. That ranking rests on replicated observational signals, one small randomized comparison, and active controlled research rather than proof of comparative efficacy. ([CIBP cohort][1]; [pilot RCT][2]; [MetAddon][6]) Full rotation and low-dose add-on therapy are separate research questions because the studies use different interventions and comparators. ([Pilot RCT][2]; [MetAddon][6])

> **Research synthesis, not prescribing guidance.** Methadone conversion is complicated by variable pharmacokinetics, delayed respiratory depression, clinically important drug interactions, QT prolongation, sedation, and withdrawal risk. Specialist selection, individualized conversion, and monitoring are required. ([FDA methadone label][9]; [international conversion guideline][8])

## What it is

Methadone is a mu-opioid receptor agonist. Pharmacology reviews also describe NMDA-receptor antagonism and inhibition of serotonin and norepinephrine uptake, while emphasizing its variable half-life and accumulation risk. ([Methadone pharmacology review][10]) Specialists may use it by fully rotating from another opioid or by adding a low dose to an existing regimen, approaches now represented by separate clinical studies. ([Pilot RCT][2]; [MetAddon][6])

Plausible explanations for improvement after full rotation include incomplete cross-tolerance, removal of the prior opioid or its metabolites, activity in mixed or neuropathic pain, methadone's non-opioid pharmacology, and specialist titration. ([Methadone pharmacology review][10]; [conventional-rotation review][11]) None of the current uncontrolled cohorts can determine which explanation caused the observed improvement, or prove that tolerance or opioid-induced hyperalgesia was present and reversed. ([CIBP cohort][1]; [US cohort][3]; [Japanese cohort][4])

## Human evidence

The strongest full-rotation signal is observational. ([CIBP cohort][1]) A 2021 retrospective cohort included 94 people with cancer-induced bone pain who completed methadone rotation. ([CIBP cohort][1]) Mean pain fell from 5.6 to 2.6, 70.2% had at least a 30% reduction, and mean daily breakthrough use fell from 3.4 to 1.8 doses. ([CIBP cohort][1])

The subsequent pilot randomized 38 people to methadone or another opioid rotation, with 29 completing 14 days. ([Pilot RCT][2]) Both groups had statistically significant reductions in average pain, worst pain, and total pain interference, with no between-group difference in satisfaction with analgesia at study end. ([Pilot RCT][2]) The study established feasibility rather than comparative efficacy and concluded that "appropriately powered multi-centre randomised controlled studies are needed." ([Pilot RCT][2])

Independent retrospective cohorts strengthen the signal without settling causality. A 27-person US cohort reported median pain falling from 7 to 4 and breakthrough use from 90 to 31 morphine milligram equivalents after at least 28 days. ([US cohort][3]) A 37-person Japanese lung-cancer cohort reported statistically significant reductions in pain and rescue-dose counts five days after methadone initiation. ([Japanese cohort][4]) Neither study included an active comparator. ([US cohort][3]; [Japanese cohort][4])

Safety and implementation are central because methadone can accumulate and cause delayed respiratory depression, QT prolongation, sedation, and clinically important interactions. ([FDA methadone label][9]; [methadone pharmacology review][10]) A 2026 multicentre retrospective cohort followed 372 hospitalized patients for 14 days after stop-and-go conversion and documented opioid-induced neurotoxicity in 70 patients, or 18.8%. ([Neurotoxicity cohort][5]) Pre-existing cognitive impairment or delirium was associated with events. ([Neurotoxicity cohort][5]) The cohort evaluated one conversion method without a control group, so its event rate should not be generalized to every methadone initiation. ([Neurotoxicity cohort][5])

The add-on lane is already in controlled testing. The public MetAddon record lists a planned sample of 166 and compares add-on methadone with add-on sustained-release morphine. ([MetAddon][6]) A separate published Chinese protocol will randomize 164 people receiving high-dose opioids to two methadone conversion strategies. ([Conversion trial][7]) By design, neither trial compares full methadone rotation against standardized conventional rotation. ([MetAddon][6]; [conversion trial][7])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

It has not failed, but the uncertainty is larger than a replication narrative suggests. The favourable full-rotation cohorts are uncontrolled, while the only randomized full-rotation comparison enrolled 38 participants and had 29 complete 14-day follow-up. ([CIBP cohort][1]; [pilot RCT][2]; [US cohort][3]; [Japanese cohort][4]) Key outcome windows were rotation completion, 14 days, 28 days, and five days, which limits conclusions about longer durability. ([CIBP cohort][1]; [pilot RCT][2]; [US cohort][3]; [Japanese cohort][4])

The decisive study would compare full methadone rotation with standardized other-opioid rotation and jointly measure pain, interference, function, scheduled and rescue opioid trajectories, toxicity, discontinuation, co-interventions, and four-to-twelve-week durability. That design follows the pilot authors' call for a powered multicentre comparison and the international guideline's finding of major evidence gaps in opioid conversion. ([Pilot RCT][2]; [international conversion guideline][8])

## Verdict

**Priority research candidate, not settled efficacy.** Replicated uncontrolled findings make a clinical signal plausible, but they have not established superiority to another opioid rotation or reversal of tolerance. ([CIBP cohort][1]; [pilot RCT][2]; [US cohort][3]; [Japanese cohort][4]) The immediate funding opportunity is protocol, recruitment, safety, and outcome work for the full-rotation comparison while coordinating with active add-on and conversion-method research. ([MetAddon][6]; [conversion trial][7]; [international conversion guideline][8])
