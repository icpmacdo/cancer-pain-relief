# Levorphanol rotation

A mechanistically interesting second-line opioid rotation with one small prospective cancer study and no active comparator. It belongs in the landscape as an exploratory option rather than a leading funding target. ([Prospective study][1]; [trial record][2])

> **Research synthesis, not a conversion protocol.** Levorphanol is a Schedule II opioid with boxed warnings for addiction, misuse, respiratory depression, accidental ingestion, and interactions with central nervous system depressants. Rotation requires specialist prescribing, individualized dosing, and monitoring. ([Levorphanol label][3])

## What it is

Levorphanol is an opioid agonist. Laboratory pharmacology also reports NMDA-receptor binding and inhibition of norepinephrine and serotonin uptake. ([Levorphanol label][3]; [pharmacology study][4]) The same laboratory study notes that these non-opioid interactions occurred at concentrations 50 to 100 times those needed to occupy opioid receptors, leaving their contribution at clinical doses uncertain. ([Pharmacology study][4])

This reported mixed pharmacology is why levorphanol is compared conceptually with methadone as a rotation option, but mechanistic resemblance does not establish comparable clinical benefit. ([Pharmacology study][4]; [prospective study][1]) Its long half-life can produce accumulation with repeated dosing, and the current label requires close monitoring during initiation and dose changes. ([Levorphanol label][3])

## Human evidence

One prospective, open-label, single-group study followed 40 cancer outpatients for 10 days after rotation. ([Prospective study][1]) Thirty-three of 40, or 82.5%, met a composite success definition, and Edmonton Symptom Assessment System and opioid-side-effect scores improved. ([Prospective study][1]) The authors described the findings as "preliminary data." ([Prospective study][1])

There was no comparator, follow-up was 10 days, and the composite endpoint did not test whether tolerance or opioid-induced hyperalgesia was present or reversed. ([Prospective study][1]) The ClinicalTrials.gov record lists the study as terminated after 43 participants were enrolled because participation was below 75%. ([Trial record][2])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

It has not been tested strongly enough to fail. The only prospective cancer study was single-centre, open-label, single-group, and short, while the registry documents early termination for low participation. ([Prospective study][1]; [trial record][2]) Those data leave multicentre recruitment, attempted-switch denominators, conversion safety, comparative benefit, and durability unresolved. ([Prospective study][1]; [trial record][2]; [levorphanol label][3])

The evidence would strengthen with a prospective multicentre registry or active-comparator study that reports every attempted switch and jointly measures pain, function, opioid burden, neurotoxicity, discontinuation, and durability. The prior registry's participation problem makes an accrual test important before a large comparative trial. ([Trial record][2])

## Verdict

**Exploratory comparator.** Include levorphanol in the clinical landscape where access and specialist expertise exist, but do not rank it above methadone or buprenorphine on one uncontrolled 40-person, 10-day study. ([Prospective study][1]; [trial record][2])
