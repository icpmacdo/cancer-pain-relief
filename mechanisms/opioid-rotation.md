# Opioid rotation

Opioid rotation, also called opioid switching, is the practice of swapping one opioid for another, or changing the route, when a patient gets inadequate analgesia or intolerable side effects on their current drug. The clinical rationale rests on incomplete cross-tolerance: because different mu-agonists engage receptors, metabolites, and genetic-polymorphism profiles slightly differently, tolerance to drug A doesn't fully transfer to drug B, so the new opioid often works at a lower-than-predicted equianalgesic dose. Rotation is needed in roughly 20-44% of cancer-pain patients and produces clinical improvement in about 40-80% of cases. In practice the new opioid's calculated dose is cut by 30-50% to account for incomplete cross-tolerance, then titrated.

## What it is

Opioid rotation is a practical cancer-pain strategy rather than a single molecular intervention. The clinician calculates an equianalgesic dose for the new opioid, reduces that calculated dose to account for incomplete cross-tolerance, and then titrates to pain relief and tolerability.

## Tractability verdict

**Active work.** Opioid rotation is already clinically used and directly relevant to the tolerance/OIH boundary. The fundable question is less whether rotation is biologically plausible and more whether better protocols, implementation support, and comparative evidence can make it safer, more reliable, and easier to deploy in cancer-pain care.
