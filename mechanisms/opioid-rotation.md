# Opioid rotation

Clinical-workaround family. Switching opioid or route exploits incomplete cross-tolerance to regain analgesia; already standard practice, which is what makes it fundable rather than speculative.

## What it is

Opioid rotation is a practical cancer-pain strategy rather than a single molecular intervention. The clinician calculates an equianalgesic dose for the new opioid, reduces that calculated dose to account for incomplete cross-tolerance, and then titrates to pain relief and tolerability.

The clinical rationale rests on incomplete cross-tolerance. Different mu-agonists engage receptors, metabolites, and genetic-polymorphism profiles slightly differently. Tolerance to drug A therefore does not fully transfer to drug B, so the new opioid often works at a lower-than-predicted equianalgesic dose.

## Human evidence

Rotation is needed in roughly 20-44% of cancer-pain patients and produces clinical improvement in about 40-80% of cases. In practice, the new opioid's calculated dose is cut by 30-50% to account for incomplete cross-tolerance, then titrated.

## Verdict

**Live ember.** Opioid rotation is already clinically used and directly relevant to the tolerance/OIH boundary. The fundable question is less whether rotation is biologically plausible and more whether better protocols, implementation support, and comparative evidence can make it safer, more reliable, and easier to deploy in cancer-pain care.
