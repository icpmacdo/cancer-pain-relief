# Conventional opioid rotation

Established clinical rescue and the necessary active comparator for methadone research. Clinical reviews and the international conversion guideline support its use when analgesia or tolerability is inadequate, but they do not establish that rotation reverses opioid tolerance. ([International guideline][1]; [cancer-pain systematic review][2])

> **Research synthesis, not a conversion protocol.** Opioid rotation requires assessment of the individual patient, comorbidities, concurrent medicines, and incomplete cross-tolerance, followed by careful dose adjustment and monitoring. ([Systematic approach][3]; [international guideline][1])

## What it is

Opioid rotation means changing from one opioid to another, or changing administration route, to improve the balance between analgesia and adverse effects. ([International guideline][1]; [cancer-pain systematic review][2]) Reviews identify incomplete cross-tolerance, interindividual variation, and pharmacokinetic differences as reasons that response can change after a switch. ([Systematic approach][3])

The benefit cannot automatically be labelled anti-tolerance. The evidence base does not isolate tolerance reversal from a one-time switching effect, removal of a poorly tolerated opioid, correction of toxicity or end-of-dose failure, treatment of a different pain component, or closer titration during the switch. ([International guideline][1]; [systematic approach][3])

## Human evidence

The 2025 MASCC, ASCO, AAHPM, HPNA, and NICSO guideline combined a systematic review of 208 eligible studies with a formal expert-consensus process. ([International guideline][1]) It reported mixed and inconclusive findings on optimal conversion approaches, modest trial strength, and substantial variability in conversion ratios, especially for methadone. ([International guideline][1]) Its summary is appropriately cautious: "The strength of the evidence from the trials is modest." ([International guideline][1])

A 2018 systematic review included nine individual studies with 725 patients and three earlier systematic reviews covering 2,296 patients. ([Cancer-pain systematic review][2]) Across the individual studies, pain control was reported through 14 days after rotation, side effects were only rarely reduced, and no particular second-line opioid was found to be best. ([Cancer-pain systematic review][2]) Because the studies varied in populations, opioids, methods, and outcomes, they do not provide one dependable efficacy estimate for rotation as a class. ([Cancer-pain systematic review][2]; [international guideline][1])

The research value of conventional rotation is therefore comparative: it is the relevant clinical control when testing whether methadone offers benefit beyond a structured switch to another opioid. ([International guideline][1]; [cancer-pain systematic review][2])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

This is not a failed intervention. The unresolved issue is comparative and mechanistic: current studies do not reliably quantify how much a standardized rotation improves pain and function, or how much improvement persists beyond short follow-up. ([International guideline][1]; [cancer-pain systematic review][2])

The evidence would strengthen with prospectively standardized protocols, raw scheduled and rescue opioid data, pain interference and function, reason-coded treatment failure, safety outcomes, and follow-up long enough to observe durability. These priorities respond directly to the guideline panel's conclusion that major clinical-practice and research gaps remain. ([International guideline][1])

## Verdict

**Established comparator.** Conventional rotation is used in cancer-pain care and should be the active control for methadone research. Its clinical role is better established than its precise comparative effect size or any claim that it reverses tolerance. ([International guideline][1]; [cancer-pain systematic review][2])
