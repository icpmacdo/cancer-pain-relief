# Ketamine

NMDA/glutamate branch. Genuinely useful in selected monitored settings, but operationally heavy and not a scalable anti-tolerance solution.

## What it is

An NMDA antagonist that can counter central sensitization/OIH and reduce opioid requirements. NMDA antagonists should, in theory, blunt the sensitization that makes opioids lose effectiveness or paradoxically worsen pain, which is why ketamine recurs alongside methadone, dextromethorphan, amantadine, and magnesium in this literature.

## Human evidence

Evidence is strongest in perioperative or selected refractory contexts. Reviews say ketamine may modulate OIH/tolerance, but evidence is insufficient for broad refractory cancer-pain use as an opioid adjunct. ([Pain Physician][1])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

*Not a failure; just bounded. It requires monitoring and has psychoactive adverse effects, so it fits better as a specialist tool than a scalable intervention. What would raise its priority: durable, tolerance-specific cancer-pain results rather than perioperative or one-time pain scores.*

## Verdict

**Adjunct.** Useful clinically, but not cheap or simple enough to be the main bet; more toolkit than scalable anti-tolerance solution, and lower-priority than methadone-centered rotation for this report's cancer-pain question.
