# Ketamine

Ketamine belongs to the NMDA/glutamate branch of the anti-tolerance landscape. This is one of the most practical-sounding mechanisms because opioid exposure can interact with glutamatergic signaling, central sensitization, and opioid-induced hyperalgesia.

## What it is

NMDA antagonist; can counter central sensitization/OIH and reduce opioid requirements.

NMDA antagonists should, in theory, blunt some of the sensitization that makes opioids lose effectiveness or paradoxically worsen pain. This is why methadone, ketamine, dextromethorphan, amantadine, and magnesium keep appearing in this literature. They are all, in different ways, connected to NMDA or excitatory signaling.

## Human evidence

Evidence is strongest in perioperative or selected refractory contexts. Reviews say ketamine may modulate OIH/tolerance, but evidence is insufficient for broad refractory cancer-pain use as an opioid adjunct. ([Pain Physician][1])

Ketamine has real clinical use, especially perioperatively and in selected refractory pain contexts, but systematic reviews remain cautious for refractory cancer pain as a broad opioid adjunct. It is not a simple global anti-tolerance solution: it requires monitoring, has psychoactive adverse effects, and fits better as a specialist tool than as a scalable palliative-care intervention.

## Tractability verdict

**Useful clinically, but not cheap/simple enough to be the main EA bet.** More protocol/toolkit than scalable anti-tolerance solution.

NMDA modulation is tractable as clinical protocol optimization, not as a generic anti-tolerance drug class. Ketamine stays in the toolkit, but it is lower-priority than methadone-centered rotation for this report's specific cancer-pain question.
