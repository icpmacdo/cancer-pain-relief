# Ketamine

A selected monitored rescue adjunct, not a broadly effective treatment for opioid-refractory cancer pain or a scalable anti-tolerance solution. The largest direct phase III test was negative, and systematic reviews continue to rate the evidence as insufficient or very uncertain. ([Phase III trial][2]; [Cochrane][3]; [2026 review][4])

> **Research synthesis, not prescribing guidance.** Ketamine use requires clinical selection and monitoring. Its prescribing information and the direct phase III cancer-pain trial document emergence reactions, cardiovascular effects, sedation-related risks, and a higher adverse-event rate with ketamine than placebo. ([Prescribing information][1]; [Phase III trial][2])

## What it is

Ketamine is a noncompetitive NMDA-receptor antagonist approved as an injectable general anaesthetic; chronic-pain use is off label. ([Prescribing information][1]; [Chronic-pain consensus][9]) Its relevance to opioid-refractory pain comes from NMDA-receptor involvement in central sensitization and the hypothesis that antagonism might reduce OIH, tolerance, or opioid requirements. ([Chronic-pain consensus][9])

## Human evidence

The largest direct cancer-pain test was negative. In a 185-person phase III placebo-controlled trial, response was 31% with ketamine and 27% with placebo (P = .55). Adverse events worse than baseline were almost twice as frequent after day 1, and the study "failed to show a net clinical benefit." ([Phase III trial][2]) Cochrane judged the cancer-pain evidence very low quality and insufficient to determine benefit and harm. ([Cochrane][3])

Newer studies do not overturn that result. A 2026 review found 12 recent palliative and end-of-life studies, including nine case reports, one cohort, one pilot feasibility study, and one prospective phase II study; it rated certainty very low. ([2026 review][4]) CIVIK reported a composite response in 10 of 20 selected terminally ill inpatients without a comparator. ([CIVIK][5]) A 34-person retrospective infusion cohort reported a mean 1.4-point pain-score reduction at 48 hours, while mean morphine-equivalent exposure remained 360 mg at baseline and 350 mg on the final infusion day. ([Infusion cohort][6])

The KETACANCER registry lists a completed prospective cohort with 81 participants and no posted results. ([KETACANCER][7]) A recruiting 24-person crossover trial tests lidocaine plus ketamine against active placebo, not ketamine alone. ([Combination trial][8]) These are results-recovery and monitoring priorities, not positive efficacy evidence.

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

The broad subcutaneous dose-escalation phase III regimen failed. ([Phase III trial][2]) That result does not prove that no prospectively defined responder subgroup or different regimen can benefit, but the recent evidence base is dominated by case reports and uncontrolled studies and therefore cannot establish such a subgroup. ([2026 review][4]; [CIVIK][5]; [Infusion cohort][6])

The verdict would strengthen only with a justified phenotype, a suitable comparator, concordant improvement in pain and function, acceptable toxicity, and a durable rather than one-time response. These requirements address the comparator, certainty, durability, and adverse-event limitations in the existing evidence. ([Phase III trial][2]; [Cochrane][3]; [2026 review][4])

## Verdict

**Selected rescue adjunct.** Ketamine can remain part of specialist practice and focused research, but current randomized and review evidence does not support it as a general anti-tolerance funding target. ([Phase III trial][2]; [Cochrane][3]; [2026 review][4]) Recover completed results and learn from active comparative work before funding another uncontrolled infusion series. ([KETACANCER][7]; [Combination trial][8])
