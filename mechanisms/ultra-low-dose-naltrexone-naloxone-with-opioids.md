# Ultra-low-dose naltrexone/naloxone with opioids

The most tempting cheap intervention in the landscape, and a warning: the candidate closest to the "cheap anti-tolerance pill" story has not become a robust clinical recommendation.

## What it is

Add a tiny amount of an opioid antagonist to an agonist, not enough to block analgesia but enough to prevent maladaptive excitatory signaling and reduce tolerance, dependence, or hyperalgesia. The counterintuitive premise is the whole appeal.

## Human evidence

Very strong rodent story: reduced tolerance/dependence and enhanced analgesia. Human data are mixed. Oxytrex (oxycodone plus ultra-low-dose naltrexone) trials showed dose-sensitive analgesic and physical-dependence signals, but effects depend on a very narrow dose window; higher "ultra-low" doses can lose the benefit. ([Sage Journals][1])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

The pattern never became a robust clinical win, and the benefit lives or dies on a very narrow dose window. The fundable next step is an expert failure-autopsy/data reanalysis, not another trial: was the dose wrong, the population wrong, or the endpoints wrong, and did the commercial program die for business reasons despite a real signal, or because the effect was too fragile? Because this is the closest thing to the imagined "cheap anti-tolerance pill," the burden of proof should be high.

## Verdict

**Watchlist.** The most interesting cheap-research bet, not ready as a recommendation. Fund the autopsy before any new trial.
