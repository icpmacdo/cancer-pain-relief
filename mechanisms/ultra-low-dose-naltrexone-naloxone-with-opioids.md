# Ultra-low-dose naltrexone/naloxone with opioids

Ultra-low-dose opioid antagonists are probably the most tempting cheap intervention in the anti-tolerance landscape. They are also a useful warning: the candidate closest to the "cheap anti-tolerance pill" story has not yet become a robust clinical recommendation.

## What it is

Tiny antagonist doses added to opioids; the "cheap miracle" candidate.

The idea is counterintuitive: add a tiny amount of an opioid antagonist to an opioid agonist, not enough to block analgesia, but enough to prevent maladaptive excitatory signaling and reduce tolerance, dependence, or hyperalgesia.

## Human evidence

Very strong rodent story: reduced tolerance/dependence and enhanced analgesia. Human data are mixed. Oxytrex trials showed dose-sensitive analgesic and physical-dependence signals, but effects depend on a very narrow dose window; higher "ultra-low" doses can lose the benefit. ([Sage Journals][1])

The animal literature looked strong. Some human trials of Oxytrex, oxycodone plus ultra-low-dose naltrexone, showed analgesic or physical-dependence signals. But the pattern never became a robust clinical win. The dose window appears narrow. Too little may do nothing; too much may start cancelling the opioid's useful effect. It is also unclear whether the most interesting result is analgesic enhancement, reduced physical dependence, reduced abuse liability, reduced tolerance, or something else.

## Tractability verdict

**Most interesting cheap-research bet**, not ready as a recommendation. Fund an expert failure-autopsy/data reanalysis before funding a new trial.

This is exactly the kind of area where the best next step may not be "fund another trial." It may be "fund an expert failure autopsy." Why did the translation stall? Was the dose wrong? Was the target population wrong? Were the endpoints wrong? Did the commercial program die for business reasons despite a real signal, or because the effect was too fragile?

For a small EA bounty/report, this is worth highlighting because it is the closest thing to the "cheap anti-tolerance pill" people imagine. But that is precisely why the burden of proof should be high.
