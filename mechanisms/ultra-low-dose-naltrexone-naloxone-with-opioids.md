# Ultra-low-dose naltrexone or naloxone with opioids

This is a low-dose oral add-on concept with positive preclinical work and mixed noncancer clinical development. Its history shows that an animal rationale and a short phase II signal did not produce a consistent development program. ([Mechanism review][5]; [Phase II trial][1]; [Published phase III trial][2]; [Development review][3])

## What it is

The strategy combines an opioid agonist with a naloxone or naltrexone dose intended to avoid blocking analgesia while interrupting a proposed switch in mu-opioid-receptor G-protein coupling. ([Mechanism review][5]) Proposed benefits include better analgesia and less tolerance, physical dependence, or hyperalgesia, but the clinical review describes mixed effects and a dose range that changes with interacting variables. ([Mechanism review][5])

## Human evidence

In a three-week phase II osteoarthritis trial, 360 adults were randomized to placebo, oxycodone, or one of two Oxytrex schedules. ([Phase II trial][1]) The paper reported, "Oxytrex bid produced a 39% reduction in pain intensity," significantly more than placebo, oxycodone four times daily, and Oxytrex four times daily. ([Phase II trial][1]) The twice-daily group received 0.002 mg naltrexone per day versus 0.004 mg in the four-times-daily group, making the signal schedule and dose dependent. ([Phase II trial][1]) This three-week noncancer trial did not establish cancer-pain durability.

A published 719-person phase III chronic-low-back-pain trial reported comparable analgesia with less study-drug use and 55% less physical dependence on its withdrawal scale for twice-daily Oxytrex versus oxycodone. ([Published phase III trial][2]) A separate 775-person phase III osteoarthritis trial, reported in a later review, found a nonsignificant 22% reduction in physical dependence amid 51% attrition. ([Development review][3]) In a double-blind crossover study of 14 experienced opioid users, adding ultra-low-dose naltrexone did not reduce oxycodone's abuse liability. ([Abuse-liability study][4]) The cited human trials involved osteoarthritis, chronic low back pain, or recreational opioid users, not cancer pain. ([Phase II trial][1]; [Published phase III trial][2]; [Development review][3]; [Abuse-liability study][4])

## Why it failed / what would change the verdict

The most useful next step is a transparent development analysis, not an immediate new efficacy trial. It should test whether the divergent findings depended on dose, schedule, population, attrition, or endpoint selection. ([Phase II trial][1]; [Published phase III trial][2]; [Development review][3]; [Mechanism review][5]) A new trial would become reasonable only if that analysis yields a prespecified dose and a clinically relevant durability endpoint, with retention strong enough to make the result interpretable.

## Verdict

**Watchlist and failure-autopsy priority.** The clinical record is mixed, entirely outside cancer pain in the cited controlled trials, and not ready to support a broad cancer-pain confirmatory trial. ([Phase II trial][1]; [Published phase III trial][2]; [Development review][3]; [Abuse-liability study][4])
